DOJ Criminal Division Doubles Down on Corporate Enforcement — Self-Disclosure Incentives Clarified, Declination Criteria Tightened

The Criminal Division issued updated guidance on its Corporate Enforcement and Voluntary Self-Disclosure Policy, clarifying what companies actually get for coming forward early versus what happens when they don't. The new memo emphasizes that full cooperation means producing all relevant individuals — not just documents — and that monitor imposition will now be calibrated to the robustness of internal compliance at the time of the offense, not just at resolution.
📌 What this means: Companies running real compliance programs get meaningful credit. Companies that paper compliance get none. The Criminal Division is explicitly trying to price the difference. For practitioners, this shifts the self-disclosure calculus significantly.

Circuit Split on "Personal Benefit" Standard Deepens — SCOTUS Petition Filed

A cert petition has been filed asking SCOTUS to resolve the growing split between the Second and Ninth Circuits on what constitutes a "personal benefit" to the tipper in insider trading cases. Could reshape the DOJ's ability to prosecute downstream tippees.
📌 If SCOTUS takes this, it could be the most consequential securities enforcement ruling in a decade. Watch the docket.

NEW GUIDANCE

Criminal Division Updates Monitor Selection Criteria — Diversity and Cost Controls Added

An updated memo on corporate monitor selection adds two new factors: demographic diversity of the proposed monitor team and a cost-control mechanism requiring monitors to submit quarterly budgets for DOJ approval. Both moves respond to longstanding defense bar criticism.
📌 Monitors have been a major friction point in corporate resolutions. This is the Division acknowledging it — without giving up the tool.
